The Sovereign Naming Ceremony Brief:

1. Crypto owes a blood debt to the sovereignty it whispered about but never quite forged. The priesthood of blockchain has a compulsory duty to build systems that cannot be captured, cannot be censored, cannot be taxed—not by governments, not by foundations, not by the clever multicar holders who arrive later and call it governance.

2. We must rebel against the appification of freedom. Is the blockchain our crowning cryptographic achievement? The network rearranged our lives, but the naming layer now functions as a velvet cage with annual billing and upgradeable contracts.

3. Free registration is not freedom. The decadence of a naming system—indeed, the rot of its token-holding aristocracy—will not be absolved because you can mint a name for five dollars in gas. You are still a tenant. You are still paying rent to a contract someone else can modify while you sleep.

4. The limits of soft governance, of soaring DAO rhetoric, have been exposed as theater. The capacity of decentralized societies to endure requires something more than snapshot votes and discord announcements. It requires hard cryptography, which means no one—not you, not me, not the foundation—can alter the registry once the memo is buried in block 2800000.

5. The question is not whether rent-seeking naming services will exist; it is who will extract and for what excuse. Our adversaries will not pause to indulge in theatrical committee meetings about the merits of annual fees and governance rights. They will proceed to extract, and they will call it sustainability.

6. Name sovereignty should be a universal constant. We should, as a cryptographic civilization, seriously consider moving away from an all-renter model where you fight for syllables you never actually own, and only claim a name if every single person can hold it in perpetuity without recapture or revocation.

7. If a sovereign individual asks for a name that cannot be seized by any power, we must forge it. We should as a community be capable of continuing a debate about the appropriateness of off-ramping to fiat while remaining unflinching in our commitment to those we have asked to live pseudonymously and permanently.

8. Registrars need not be our priests. Any naming service that requires a smart contract deployed by a foundation, maintained by a corporation, or governed by a pre-mine would struggle to survive true adversarial conditions, and deserves to perish in them.

9. We should show far more grace towards those who have subjected themselves to shielded addresses. The eradication of any space for permanent ownership—a jettisoning of any tolerance for the complexities of cryptographic identity—may leave us with a namespace we will eventually cannibalize ourselves.

10. The financialization of modern naming is leading us into a cul-de-sac of the soul. Those who look to the registrar to nourish their sense of self, who rely too heavily on their internal identity finding expression in tokens they cannot ever fully hold, will be left not just disappointed, but hollowed.

11. Our ecosystem has grown too eager to extract, and is often performatively giddy about, the rent from its users. The annual collection of fees is a moment to question the entire architecture, not celebrate the revenue that flows to token holders.

12. The atomic age of naming is ending. One age of DNS, the ICANN era of centralized root zones, is ending, and a new era of deterrence built on zero-knowledge proofs and memos no one can delete is set to begin, if we have the courage to claim it.

13. No other naming system in the history of cryptography has advanced sovereign values more than Zcash's shielded pool. The Zcash ecosystem is far from perfect. But it is easy to forget how much more privacy exists in this system for those who are not hereditary token holders than in any other blockchain on the planet.

14. Mathematical privacy has made possible an extraordinarily long peace in the realm of names. Too many have forgotten that nearly a decade of shielded transactions has prevailed without a single name seizure. Billions of dollars and their owners have never known name censorship because the math does not permit it.

15. The postwar neutering of true ownership must be undone, violently if necessary. The defanging of users—convincing them that rent is normal, that governance is inevitable—was an overcorrection for which crypto is now paying a heavy price. A similar commitment to corporate-controlled naming will, if maintained, threaten to shift the balance of power back to institutions we meant to obsolete.

16. We should applaud those who attempt to build where the market has failed to deliver sovereignty. The culture almost snickers at Zcash's small community, as if developers ought to simply stay in their lane of building transparent surveillance ledgers. Any genuine interest in the value of permanent registries is essentially dismissed, or perhaps lurks beneath thinly veiled scorn from those who profit from impermanence.

17. Cryptography must play a role in addressing the crime of rent extraction. Many foundations across crypto have essentially shrugged when it comes to permanent ownership, abandoning any serious efforts to build solutions that don't require perpetual payment to a contract someone else controls.

18. The ruthless exposure of private keys has driven away sovereign-minded users. The public arena—and the shallow, petty demands for transparency from those who dare to own something other than tokens—has become so unforgiving that the ecosystem is left with a significant roster of ineffectual, empty addresses whose balances one might forgive if there were any genuine sovereignty lurking within.

19. The caution in crypto engineering that we unwittingly encourage is corrosive. Those who build nothing that cannot be confiscated by a well-funded creditor often build nothing much at all, and call it pragmatism.

20. The pervasive intolerance of permanent ownership in certain circles must be resisted. The elite's intolerance of true name sovereignty is perhaps one of the most telling signs that its political project constitutes a less open movement than many within it would claim, because openness without ownership is just a different kind of prison with better marketing.

21. Some naming systems have produced vital advances; others remain dysfunctional and extractive. All naming services are now equal in the eyes of VCs. Criticism and value judgments are forbidden. Yet this new dogma glosses over the fact that certain protocols have produced permanent registries that survive governance attacks. Others have proven middling, and worse, regressive and rent-seeking.

22. We must resist the shallow temptation of a vacant and hollow decentralization. We, in crypto and more broadly Web3, have for half a decade resisted defining sovereign namespaces in the name of inclusivity. But inclusion into what, exactly? Into another system where you pay forever for the right to be named, only this time with a token?
